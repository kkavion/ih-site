Sundarbans

Sundarbans invites you to explore the jungle, various species of animals both unqiue and endangered, the tribal life and an exclusive solidarity with nature’s bounties.

Feast your eyes on serene expanses of green forests and vast azure waters at the isles of Sundarbans. The largest mangrove forest of the world is a resplendent sight with its majestic flora and fauna, as well as the raw appeal of the untouched hinterlands. The Sundarbans, adorning the southern stretch of West Bengal tempts you with its unadulterated natural bounty that soothes your weary soul. The Sunderban National Park boasts multiple accolades as a Tiger Reserve, a biosphere, a UNESCO world heritage site, and as the abode of a mesmerizing range of wildlife, from the most popular Royal Bengal tiger to innumerable birds and reptiles. Named after the “Sundari” trees that encircle the dusky islands, Sundarbans has been charmingly dubbed the “beautiful forest” and you can discover why!

Trivia

It is an unbelievable fact that the Sundarbans of India cover an area that is ten times the size of Venice!

Indulgence

Sundarbans is a place that will tempt and prompt you to explore. The boats sail deep into creeks until you are close enough to touch land. To sit with cameras poised, eyes peeled and ears cocked, and suddenly spot a wild animal just carelessly appearing before you will uniquely combine the backwater experience commendable with that of the forest.

Best time to visit

The winter months from October to March constitute the peak tourist season when the temperatures are comfortable enough for you to explore the delta at large. It is also the ideal time to view migratory birds, or Olive Ridley turtles and crocodiles. Summer is considered the best time for safaris as wildlife can often be found quenching their thirst in open ponds.

Legacy of the World's Largest Mangrove

Sundarbans, West Bengal 743370

Until the nineteenth century when the British mapped the landscape, large parts of the forest remained uncharted, unexplored, and enshrouded in woody darkness, large parts of it were declared as reserve forests. Slowly the Sundarbans you know today emerged as a wildlife sanctuary in 1977 and then as a national park in 1984.

Good To Know

Today you can boast about visiting a world heritage site as declared by UNESCO and happily look forward to spotting Royal Bengal Tigers behind the billowy curtains of mangroves!

The culture of the Sundarbans is exceptionally beautiful for their sense of unity and courage. As the local inhabitants head into the dangerous depths of forest and sea their lives are shaped by the necessity for survival. Monsoon June - September

Monsoons straddle the line between disaster and rejuvenation for the delta. With the rising water level and the threat of submerging islands, heavy rains are feared by the locals. The river usually overflows which makes travel next to impossible, and access to the wildlife parks and sanctuaries is completely shut off.

Good To Know

This is a time when nature rejoices as temperatures dip down to 25-30 degrees Celsius. The mangroves and other flora grow abundantly lush and thick and the wildlife also enters their breeding season.

Local Transport Motor Boat

Riverine waterways are the exciting mode of transport here. Since the Sundarbans are spread across more than fifty islands, the only way to reach them is by water. It makes for a memorable experience as you travel from one tourist spot to another by motorboats or launches which are specially designed to be extremely quiet to avoid spooking the wild animals. The boats also take you close to banks where crocodiles or turtles rest and you may even find the bumpy hide of a reptile right next to your boat's hull.
